## Beat 1 — Hook

The one screenshot-worth idea: **two billionaires who co-founded OpenAI together
are now in federal court fighting over a single question — did Elon Musk's early
donation legally bind OpenAI to stay a nonprofit forever?** Musk says he was
"duped" into bankrolling what became a ~$800B+ company; OpenAI says no such
promise was ever made and Musk is a sore-loser competitor. As of mid-May 2026
the trial is in closing arguments in Oakland.

The case is *Musk v. Altman*, tried before U.S. District Judge Yvonne Gonzalez
Rogers in the Northern District of California (Oakland). [Wikipedia, "Musk v.
Altman"; CNBC, 2026-04-27]

## Beat 2 — Evidence

- Musk co-founded OpenAI in **2015** alongside Sam Altman and Greg Brockman as a
  nonprofit AI lab "for the benefit of humanity." [NPR, 2026-05-12; CNBC]
- Musk donated roughly **$44 million** to OpenAI between 2016 and 2020
  (Wikipedia's figure); at trial Musk testified he gave "$38 million of
  essentially free funding." The ~$38M figure is the one OpenAI and the court
  filings use as the donation at issue. [Wikipedia; MIT Technology Review,
  2026-05-01; CNBC, 2026-05-12]
- That funding, Musk testified, "they then used to create what would become an
  $800 billion company." OpenAI President Greg Brockman cited a valuation of
  **"more than $850 billion"** from the stand. [MIT Tech Review; CNBC,
  2026-05-13]
- Musk is seeking damages **exceeding $130 billion** (some filings cited ~$134
  billion), plus the unwinding of OpenAI's October 2025 corporate restructuring
  and disgorgement of gains from the Microsoft partnership. [CNBC, 2026-04-24;
  Let's Data Science]
- OpenAI's October 2025 restructuring: it formed **OpenAI Group PBC**, a public
  benefit corporation. The original nonprofit (OpenAI Foundation) received a
  **26%** ownership stake; **Microsoft received 27%**. The nonprofit retains
  board-level control. [Wikipedia; CNBC]
- Trial logistics: jury selection began **April 27, 2026**; a **nine-person
  advisory jury** was seated; opening arguments began April 28. Musk testified
  April 28–30. Closing arguments were scheduled for Thursday, **May 14, 2026**;
  the liability phase is expected to wrap by **May 21**. [CNBC, 2026-04-27;
  CNBC, 2026-05-12; ABC7]

## Beat 3 — Mechanism

**How the case works — the legal crux.** The central question the jury and
judge must answer: did Musk's ~$38M donation create a **charitable trust** —
a legal obligation binding OpenAI to remain a nonprofit pursuing its founding
mission — and if so, did Altman and Brockman breach it by converting to a
for-profit structure? [CNBC, 2026-05-13; NPR]

**Claims at issue.** After the judge trimmed the suit on May 1, 2025 (excluding
false-advertising and breach-of-fiduciary-duty claims), the surviving claims
that went to trial are **fraud** and **unjust enrichment**. [Wikipedia]

**Two-phase structure.** The trial is split into (1) a **liability phase** — did
wrongdoing occur — and (2) a **remedies phase** — damages and what to do about
it. The nine-person jury weighs in **only on liability, and only in an advisory
capacity**: Judge Gonzalez Rogers can accept or override the jury and makes the
final decision in both phases. She decides remedies on her own. [CNBC,
2026-04-27; CNBC, 2026-05-13]

**Case timeline.**
1. **Feb 29, 2024** — Musk sues OpenAI, Altman, and Brockman in San Francisco
   Superior Court (state): breach of contract, promissory estoppel.
2. **Mid-2024** — Musk withdraws the state suit.
3. **August 2024** — Musk refiles in federal court (N.D. Cal), with expanded
   claims including RICO, fraud, and unjust enrichment.
4. **Nov 2024** — Musk moves for a preliminary injunction to block the
   for-profit conversion.
5. **Feb/March 2025** — Judge Gonzalez Rogers denies the injunction (calls
   Musk's "irreparable harm" claim "a stretch") but offers an expedited trial.
6. **April 2025** — OpenAI countersues Musk, alleging his actions are bad-faith
   tactics to harm a competitor.
7. **May 1, 2025** — Judge narrows the case to fraud + unjust enrichment.
8. **May 5, 2025** — OpenAI says it will no longer fully spin the for-profit
   away from nonprofit control; Musk's lawyer says the suit continues anyway.
9. **October 2025** — OpenAI forms OpenAI Group PBC (nonprofit 26%, Microsoft
   27%).
10. **April 27, 2026** — Trial begins in Oakland.
[Wikipedia; CNBC, 2026-01-08; CNBC, 2026-04-07]

## Beat 4 — Comparison

**Musk's account vs. OpenAI's account** — the two sides describe the same facts
in incompatible ways:

| | Musk's case | OpenAI's defense |
|---|---|---|
| The donation | A charitable gift that created a binding trust | A donation with no strings; no enforceable promise |
| The founding deal | A vow OpenAI would stay nonprofit "in perpetuity" | "There were never any promises made that could form the charitable trust" (OpenAI attorney Savitt) |
| Musk's 2018 exit | He left over disagreements but kept faith in the mission | He "gave up" and "left the nonprofit for dead" after being denied control (Altman, Brockman) |
| Musk's motive | Protecting humanity from unsafe, profit-driven AI | "Sour grapes" — Musk runs rival xAI and wants to damage a competitor |
| The restructuring | "Looting the nonprofit"; a "bait and switch" | A necessary structure to raise capital; nonprofit still controls it |

OpenAI's framing leans hard on Musk being a **direct competitor**: Musk admitted
on the stand that his company xAI "partly" distills OpenAI's models (he defended
it as "standard practice to use other AIs to validate your AI" — the admission
reportedly drew gasps in the courtroom). [MIT Tech Review, 2026-05-01; CNBC,
2026-05-12; CNBC, 2026-05-13]

## Beat 5 — Counter

**Honest steelman / weaknesses on each side.**

Against Musk:
- He admitted he wanted **control** of OpenAI in 2015–2018 (including a possible
  merger into Tesla) and left when he didn't get it — undercutting the "pure
  mission" narrative. [CNBC, 2026-05-13; Brockman testimony]
- He runs **xAI**, a direct competitor, and admitted distilling OpenAI's models
  — feeding OpenAI's "sour grapes" defense. [MIT Tech Review]
- The judge already called his "irreparable harm" claim "a stretch" when denying
  the injunction. [Wikipedia]
- He texted Brockman about a **settlement two days before trial** — read by some
  as a sign of a weak hand. [CNBC, 2026-05-04]
- He flew to China as the trial neared its end, after the judge warned he could
  be recalled to court. [CBS News]

Against OpenAI / Altman:
- Altman's **credibility** is squarely on trial. Musk's lawyers cross-examined
  him using prior criticism from former OpenAI figures — Ilya Sutskever, Mira
  Murati, Helen Toner — about a "pattern of deception."
- Sutskever testified he spent **months gathering evidence** of what he called
  Altman's pattern of deception and poor management, tied to the November 2023
  board ouster of Altman. [CNBC, 2026-05-13]
- OpenAI did, in fact, move from a pure nonprofit toward a capped/for-profit
  structure with a $13B+ Microsoft partnership — the factual core of Musk's
  "bait and switch" claim is not in dispute; only its legal meaning is.
- Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella testified he was **never told why Altman was
  fired** in November 2023 — reinforcing the governance-instability theme.
  [CNBC, 2026-05-11]

The advisory-jury structure is itself a counterweight: even a jury verdict for
Musk can be overridden by the judge, and vice versa. [CNBC, 2026-05-13]

## Beat 6 — Application

**How to read the outcome as it lands:**
- **Watch the two phases separately.** A liability finding for Musk does not
  mean he gets $130B or a corporate unwind — remedies are a separate fight,
  decided by the judge alone.
- **Treat the jury verdict as advisory, not final.** Judge Gonzalez Rogers can
  accept, modify, or override it. The headline number from the jury is not the
  ruling.
- **The real stakes are structural, not just monetary.** Musk wants OpenAI's
  October 2025 PBC conversion unwound and (per an April 2026 filing) Altman
  ousted — outcomes that would reshape who controls a leading AI lab.
- **The precedent question:** whether a donation to a nonprofit can later be
  re-cast as a binding "charitable trust" has implications well beyond OpenAI.

## Beat 7 — Close

Candidate one-sentence takeaways:
- "The trial isn't really about $130 billion — it's about whether a donation can
  legally freeze a company's mission forever."
- "Two co-founders, one founding promise, and no agreement on whether the
  promise was ever made."
- "Musk wants to prove he was robbed of a charity; OpenAI wants to prove he's
  just a competitor with regrets."
- "An advisory jury, a two-phase trial, and a judge with the final word — the
  verdict will be loud, but it won't be the last word."
